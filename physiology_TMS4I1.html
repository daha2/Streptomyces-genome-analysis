Morphological and Physiological Characterization of Streptomyces silvae TMS4I1

This page summarizes the morphological and physiological characterization of S. silvae TMS4I1 based on experimental analyses performed during the dissertation project.

The presented data combine colony morphology, scanning electron microscopy, and physiological stress assays in order to characterize growth behavior, morphological traits, and environmental adaptability of the strain.

Key findings. S. silvae TMS4I1 shows characteristic streptomycete morphology with differentiated colony structures and filamentous hyphae. In addition, the strain displays broad physiological adaptability, including tolerance to salt, pH variation, and lysozyme, together with pronounced β-hemolytic activity.

Macro- and Micromorphology

Colony morphology was documented on standardized media in order to capture medium-dependent variation in growth pattern, pigmentation, and aerial mycelium formation. Scanning electron microscopy was used to visualize filamentous growth and microstructural features of the strain.

Cultivation was carried out on ISP2–ISP7 and synthetic media, both with and without L-tyrosine (Suter, 1978). The panels show colony morphology after 14 days of incubation at 28°C. (B) Scanning electron micrograph of S. silvae TMS4I1 after 10 days of incubation on MYM agar at 28°C.

Macromorphology

Colony morphology varies substantially depending on the cultivation medium, including differences in pigmentation, surface texture, and colony architecture.

Micromorphology

SEM imaging reveals typical filamentous hyphae and differentiated streptomycete morphology at the microscopic level.

Physiological Characterization and Stress Tolerance

To assess ecological adaptability and stress response, the strain was tested under a range of environmental conditions including salt stress, lysozyme exposure, temperature variation, and different pH conditions.

Figure 2. Physiological characterization and stress tolerance of S. silvae TMS4I1. Unless otherwise indicated, all assays were performed in basal medium 5339 and cultures were incubated for 14 days at 28°C.

(A) Salt tolerance: growth at increasing NaCl concentrations (0, 2.5, 5, 7.5, and 10%);
(B) Hemolysis: β-hemolytic activity on Columbia blood agar, indicated by a clear halo around colonies;
(C) Lysozyme resistance: growth at increasing concentrations of lysozyme (0, 10, 25, 50, 75, and 100 µg/ml);
(D) Temperature optimum: colony growth on MYM agar incubated at constant temperatures ranging from 7 to 37°C;
(E) pH tolerance: growth at pH values from 2 to 10, with incubation on a shaker at 90 rpm.

Environmental tolerance

The strain maintains growth across a broad range of salt concentrations, temperatures, and pH conditions, indicating substantial ecological flexibility.

Stress response

Resistance to lysozyme and pronounced β-hemolytic activity further emphasize the robust physiological profile of TMS4I1.

Interpretation. The combined morphological and physiological characterization of S. silvae TMS4I1 shows that the strain displays both characteristic streptomycete colony and hyphal morphology as well as a broad tolerance towards different environmental conditions. The strain shows growth across a wide range of salt concentrations, pH conditions, and temperatures, and also displays resistance to lysozyme as well as pronounced β-hemolytic activity. Together, these observations support the view that strain TMS4I1 is physiologically adaptable and well suited to variable environmental conditions.
