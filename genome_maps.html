Genome Architecture of Streptomyces silvae Strains

This page presents circular genome maps and selected comparative features of four strains within the S. silvae clade. Together, the figures summarize contig organization, structural genome characteristics, functional annotation patterns, and assembly statistics.

These visualizations provide a genome-wide comparison of conserved and variable properties across the analyzed strains.

Key findings. The analyzed genomes show highly similar genome sizes (approximately 8.1–8.3 Mb) and nearly identical GC contents (~71.3–71.43%), supporting their classification within the same species. At the same time, differences in assembly contiguity, functional gene distribution, and biosynthetic repertoires reveal meaningful intraspecific variation.

Strains Included

  • Streptomyces silvae TMS12I2
  • Streptomyces silvae TMS4I1
  • Streptomyces silvae For3T – type strain
  • Streptomyces sp. M3

Genome Statistics and Assembly Quality

Comparative genome analysis revealed highly similar genome sizes and GC contents across all four strains, consistent with their classification within the species S. silvae. Despite this overall similarity, the assemblies differ in contiguity and annotation metrics. Strain M3 shows the highest assembly continuity, whereas the remaining strains are more fragmented. In addition, variation in tRNA and rRNA gene copy numbers indicates functional differences that may reflect strain-specific adaptation.

Interpretation of genome architecture. The combined structural and functional analyses show that the four strains are genomically closely related, yet not identical. Similar genome size, GC content, and broad functional profiles support their close taxonomic relationship. At the same time, differences in assembly contiguity, tRNA/rRNA copy number, and selected functional categories indicate intraspecific diversification. In particular, strain TMS4I1 displays elevated gene numbers in several metabolic categories, suggesting strain-specific adaptation, whereas TMS12I2, For3T, and M3 remain more similar to one another.

Interpretation of BGCs. The BGC comparison reveals a highly conserved core biosynthetic repertoire across the four analyzed strains. All four genomes contain fully identical clusters for alkylresorcinol, desferrioxamine B, ectoine, isorenieratene, and melanin. In addition, ten further clusters are shared by all strains and show similarities to known reference clusters ranging from very low correspondence, such as 3% for tetronasin and lactonamycin, to markedly higher similarity values such as 90% for coelichelin.

Despite this broad conservation, divergence between the strains is also evident. Hopene-associated clusters occur in all four genomes, but their similarity differs, reaching 76% in TMS12I2 and For3T and 84% in TMS4I1 and M3. The clearest distinction is visible in the genomic region positioned approximately between 4 and 5 o’clock in the circular representation. At nearly corresponding loci, antiSMASH assigned different BGC classes, reflected by different colors in the circular plot. Closer inspection showed that the cluster in strain TMS12I2 lacks the genes associated with butyrolactone biosynthesis. Accordingly, the TMS12I2 cluster displayed a 77% similarity to the maduralactomycin A cluster, whereas the corresponding clusters in TMS4I1, For3T, and M3 showed initial similarities of 25–33% to prejadomycin. Additional BLAST- and subcluster-based analyses nevertheless demonstrated that these loci still retain high relatedness overall, including 86% similarity to maduralactomycin A in TMS4I1 and M3, 72% in For3T, and strong agreement with landomycin/simocyclinone-associated regions.

Further strain-specific differences are also apparent. Strain TMS12I2 contains a unique cluster with 8% similarity to an arseno-polyketide, whereas clusters related to stenothricin and griseobactin, present in the other strains, are absent. Strain TMS4I1, in turn, contains additional unique clusters showing similarity to kinamycin (25%) and nataxazole (7%). A cluster weakly related to hexacosalactone A occurs in all four strains at about 4% similarity, while M3 contains an additional cluster with a 6% similarity to the same reference. Overall, the results indicate that BGC divergence within the S. silvae clade is real but moderate: despite locus-specific variation and differences in annotation details, the strains retain a strongly conserved shared biosynthetic backbone.
