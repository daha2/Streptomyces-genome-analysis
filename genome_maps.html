Genome Architecture of Streptomyces silvae Strains

This page presents circular genome maps of four strains within the Streptomyces silvae clade. The figure summarizes genome architecture, contig organization, distribution of coding sequences, non-coding RNA genes, antibiotic resistance determinants, GC skew, and GC content.

Together, these maps provide a genome-wide visual comparison of structural organization and annotated functional features across the analyzed strains.

Key findings. The circular genome maps show a broadly conserved genomic architecture across the S. silvae clade. At the same time, variation in contig organization, gene distribution, and compositional features suggests strain-specific differences in genomic organization and functional potential.

Strains included

  • Streptomyces silvae #12I2
  • Streptomyces silvae #4I1
  • Streptomyces silvae For3T – type strain
  • Streptomyces sp. M3

Circular Genome Maps

Figure 1. Genome maps of the four Streptomyces silvae strains #12I2, #4I1, For3 and M3. The concentric circles arranged from the outside to the inside show, for each genome, the contig lengths in megabase pairs (Mbp) in the first ring, followed by a strain-specific colour coding of the contigs in the second ring. Contigs with a length of < 149 kbp were merged and are shown in white.

The third and fourth rings display the positions of protein-coding genes on the forward and reverse strands, coloured according to their functional categories. The fifth ring marks the localisation of tRNAs and rRNAs, followed by the sixth ring showing antibiotic resistance genes.

The innermost three rings represent positive GC skew, negative GC skew, and GC content.

Methods

  • Genome scaffolding: RagTag
  • Reference genome: Streptomyces sp. M3
  • Genome annotation: RASTtk
  • Genome visualization: Circos

Interpretation

The genome maps illustrate the conserved genomic architecture within the Streptomyces silvae clade. While the overall structure appears similar across strains, variations in gene distribution and compositional patterns can be observed along the chromosome, reflecting differences in gene content and potential metabolic capabilities.
